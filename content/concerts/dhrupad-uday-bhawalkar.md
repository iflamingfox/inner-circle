dhrupadBody: |
  Dhrupad is the oldest surviving form of Indian classical music. Its roots run back
  to *Samagana* — the chanting of the Sama Veda — and its name joins *dhruva*
  (constant, structured) with *pada* (word). Born in temple and court, refined under
  Raja Man Singh of Gwalior some six centuries ago, it has been carried into the
  present by the Dagar family.

  A Dhrupad recital unfolds slowly and without hurry: a long, meditative *alaap*
  that lets a raga reveal itself note by note, before the entrance of the pakhawaj
  and a composition set to rhythmic cycle. Through it all the raga is kept pure, and
  beneath the intricate rhythm runs a profound, unbroken sense of devotion.

Dhrupad offers more than a captivating performance — it is a portal to a profound
inner world. This millennia-old art form weaves a tapestry of serenity,
contemplation, and ecstasy, and each meticulously crafted note is an invitation to
sit still and explore the depths of your own being.

On **July 5th**, Pandit Uday Bhawalkar performs an intimate recital, accompanied by
**Sri Pratap Awad** on the pakhawaj — the double-headed, barrel-shaped drum that has
partnered Dhrupad for centuries — and his senior disciple **Sri Kirit Singh**. Close
your eyes, and let an ancient raga carry you somewhere quiet.

For those who wish to go further, the recital opens a **three-day workshop** (July
6–8). Step by step, and with no prior experience required, you will learn the core
elements of the Dhrupad sound — voice, syllables, tonal modulation, breath, and the
traditional approach to unfolding a raga — until, by the end, you have sung a full
raga yourself.
